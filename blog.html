School Programs


Lecture Series on Science and Technology in Schools

This Initiative of TOE organization focuses on aware and teaching school students about modern aspects of Science and Technology with major focus on the field of Astronomy.

There are different series of lectures and Interactive session which covers topics like: Introduction to Remote Sensing, Climate change monitoring, Basic Astronomy knowledge and many more are included in the Module which also provide students’ the exposure to many space innovation based startups like SDNX, SPACE India. We also introduce secondary school students to the different career paths they can choose in their graduation and work towards innovation and Research.

Lecture by Ann Devereaux - Division Manager for Systems Engineering, Jet Propulsion Laboratory on What could the future hold?” at American Center, New Delhi

Ann Devereaux is Division Manager for the Jet Propulsion Laboratory’s (JPL’s) Systems Engineering Division, which includes both project and spacecraft systems engineering from proposal formulation and initial design through integration, test, and launch operations. Ann served as lead spacecraft systems engineer for Mars Science Laboratory (MSL) as well as most recently for the Mars 2020 rover in development. Prior to that, she has over 20 years of experience with both technology demonstrations and flight programs, designing and building custom high-reliability electronics for radios and instruments. Ann was the lead electronics designer for the original Electra relay radios (both orbiter and lander versions), used on multiple Mars missions to date including Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO) and the Curiosity rover.

Exploring the Dark Universe – University of Chicago Center, New Delhi

Much of what we know about the universe comes from direct observations of starlight. As increasingly powerful telescopes reveal fainter and more distant objects, tremendous progress has been made over the last decade in mapping the large-scale matter distribution across the cosmos.

The vast majority of matter (i.e., 90%) does not shine like stars. Exploring this uncharted component of the universe requires unconventional techniques that go beyond traditional observations. In this talk, Professor Hsiao-Wen Chen from the Department of Astrophysics & Astronomy at the University of Chicago described new ways that astronomers are using to explore this dark universe.
